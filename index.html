RQ: To what extent does increasing NaCl concentration (0%, 1%, 2%, 3%, 4%) affect the fermentation rate of yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae), as measured by the volume of carbon dioxide produced?

Background Information

Fermentation is an anaerobic metabolic process used by yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) to generate ATP when oxygen is scarce. In this pathway, glucose undergoes glycolysis to form pyruvate, which is subsequently converted into ethanol and carbon dioxide (CO2). This process is catalyzed by specific enzymes, such as zymase. Consequently, the rate of fermentation is highly dependent on environmental factors that influence enzyme kinetics, including temperature, pH, and substrate concentration.

Sodium chloride (NaCl) influences yeast fermentation through two primary mechanisms: osmotic stress and specific ion toxicity. Firstly, adding NaCl lowers the water potential ($\Psi$) of the external solution. This creates a hypertonic environment, driving water out of the yeast cells via osmosis, which leads to a loss of turgor pressure and potential plasmolysis (cell shrinkage). Secondly, high intracellular concentrations of Na+ and Cl- ions can function as toxic inhibitors. These ions can interfere with the electrostatic interactions within enzyme structures, leading to conformational changes (denaturation) or blocking active sites, thereby reducing the efficiency of glycolysis and fermentation. (Belz et al., 2017)

Hypotheses

Research Hypothesis: An increase in sodium chloride (NaCl) concentration will significantly decrease the rate of fermentation in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, as measured by a reduction in carbon dioxide production volume.

Null Hypothesis: Changes in sodium chloride (NaCl) concentration will have no significant effect on the fermentation rate of Saccharomyces cerevisiae.

Methodology Critiques & Adjustments

Note on Temperature: The experiment was conducted at 45°C. While this facilitates a rapid reaction initially, it is important to note that the optimal growth temperature for S. cerevisiae is typically 30–35°C. At 45°C, the yeast may experience heat stress in addition to osmotic stress, potentially accelerating enzyme denaturation. Future iterations of this experiment should consider a lower controlled temperature (e.g., 35°C) to isolate the variable of salinity.
